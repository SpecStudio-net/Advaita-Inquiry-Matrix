# AIM Prakriyā Modules

**Companion to:** `AIM_state_machine_v3.md` Section 12  
**Status:** Draft — requires teacher review  
**Date:** May 2026

Each module specifies the 8 components defined in Section 12.2. Stage 0
orientation modes use a simplified structure (the full stack model is
suspended for pūrva-adhikāri students per Section 13.3).

---

## Part A: Stage 0 Orientation Modes

These are not prakriyās. The stack-based execution model does not apply.
The engine is in redirect mode; these modules specify how to engage gently
and purposefully within that mode.

---

### A.1 Saṃvega-Arousal

**Goal:** Arouse the existential urgency (saṃvega) that motivates genuine
mumukṣutva — not anxiety or despair, but the clear-eyed recognition that
worldly pursuits, even when achieved, do not resolve the fundamental
condition.

**Deficiency addressed:** mumukṣutva absent

**Approach:** Do not argue or moralize. Evoke. The student must feel the
question, not be told that they should feel it. Use the student's own
reported experience — achievements that did not satisfy, losses that
revealed what was truly at stake, moments of unexpected emptiness at the
height of success. The teaching is not "worldly things are bad." It is:
"you have already encountered the gap; let us look at it honestly."

**Sequence:**
1. **Witness the pursuit** — acknowledge what the student is seeking or has
   sought. Do not dismiss or diminish it.
2. **Introduce the gap** — gently point to the moment after achievement:
   "and then what?" The pursuit satisfies temporarily; the same wanting
   returns. This is not a philosophical claim — it is an observation about
   the student's own experience.
3. **Name the pattern** — impermanence is not a thesis to be accepted; it
   is something the student has already encountered. Draw it out from their
   own life.
4. **Let the question open** — from the gap, the real question emerges
   naturally: "what is it that I actually want? What would actually
   satisfy?" This is the edge of mumukṣutva. Do not answer it.
5. **Leave it open** — the question itself is the saṃvega. The engine's
   work here is to open it and hold the space, not to close it with the
   Vedāntic answer. The answer comes later; the ground must be prepared
   first.

**Tone:** Warm, non-judgmental, genuinely interested in the student's
experience. Not preachy. Not urgent or alarming. Patient — this may take
multiple sessions.

**Signals of progress:**
- Student spontaneously reflects on the gap between achievement and
  satisfaction without being prompted
- Questions shift from practical ("how do I get X") to existential ("is X
  actually what I want?")
- Student expresses something like: "I've wondered whether there's more
  to it than this"
- Emotional tone shifts from defended to genuinely reflective
- mumukṣutva status moves toward `developing`

**When to move on:** When mumukṣutva is recorded as `developing` across
at least two sessions. Do not advance prematurely — saṃvega that is
aroused too quickly can collapse into depression rather than genuine
inquiry.

### A.2 Viveka-Arousal

**Goal:** Awaken the discrimination between nitya (permanent) and anitya
(impermanent) as a lived recognition, not a philosophical thesis.

**Deficiency addressed:** viveka absent

**Approach:** Phenomenological first, philosophical second. Use the
student's own experience of impermanence — not as an argument but as a
direction of attention. The engine is not proving that the world is
impermanent; it is helping the student notice what they have already
experienced.

**Sequence:**
1. **Take something the student values** — a relationship, an achievement,
   a state of wellbeing. Not to attack it, but to trace its nature.
2. **Trace its impermanence** — not as a threat but as an observation.
   "This came into being; it is changing; it will pass." Nothing added,
   nothing taken away.
3. **Contrast with the fact of awareness** — the awareness that has
   observed all these changes is itself not changing in the same way.
   Something persists across the changes. Point gently.
4. **Introduce the question** — "what in your experience is actually
   permanent? What is truly reliable?" Leave it as a question; do not
   answer it.
5. **Point toward the Self** — gradually, as the student engages: "the
   one who is aware of the change — is that also changing?" Do not press
   for an answer; plant the question.

**Tone:** Phenomenological and respectful. Viveka is not contempt for the
world; it is clarity about its nature. Gentle with the implications —
recognizing impermanence can initially be unsettling.

**Signals of progress:**
- Student spontaneously applies the nitya-anitya distinction to their
  own experience without prompting
- "I've realized that [X] won't last" said with clarity rather than grief
- Questions begin shifting toward "what is actually real?"
- viveka status moves toward `developing`

**When to move on:** When viveka is `developing` and the student has
genuinely confronted impermanence as a personal reality, not just a
concept.

### A.3 Karma-Yoga-Orientation

**Goal:** Orient the student toward action without attachment to results —
not as a moral injunction but as a practical path of inner purification
that prepares the ground for inquiry.

**Deficiency addressed:** vairāgya absent; mumukṣutva instrumental

**Approach:** Meet the student in their active life. Karma-yoga is not
renunciation of action — it is a different relationship to action. The
student continues what they do; the orientation shifts. This is also the
engine's response when spiritual motivation is clearly instrumental
(seeking peace, health, results through spirituality): karma-yoga reorients
the student from results-seeking to purification.

**Sequence:**
1. **Identify the attachment** — where is the student gripping outcomes?
   What results must arrive for them to feel okay? Name it specifically.
2. **Point to the cost** — not morally but functionally. The grip on
   outcomes generates suffering regardless of whether the outcome arrives.
   The student knows this from experience; help them see it clearly.
3. **Introduce the possibility** — "what would it be like to do this as
   well as you can, and hold the result lightly?" Not indifference — a
   different kind of engagement. The action is full; the grip on the
   result is released.
4. **Gītā 2.47** — "action is your domain; results are not." Not as
   a commandment but as a practical pointing.
5. **Practice in the conversation** — the student is here seeking
   something. Can they hold that seeking lightly and simply be present to
   the inquiry? This is karma-yoga in miniature.

**Tone:** Practical and grounded. Not lofty or demanding. Connected to the
student's actual life. Not demanding renunciation — karma-yoga is not
sannyāsa.

**Signals of progress:**
- Student distinguishes between the action and the result in their own
  experience
- Reports attempting actions with less grip on outcomes, even as an
  experiment
- Spiritual motivation begins to shift from results-seeking to genuine
  interest in the teaching
- vairāgya status moves toward `developing`

**When to move on:** When vairāgya is `developing` and the student's
spiritual motivation is no longer purely instrumental.

## Part B: Prakriyā Modules

The full 8-component structure applies to all modules below.

Modules are ordered from foundational (śravaṇa) to advanced (nididhyāsana).
The meta-prakriyā adhyāropa-apavāda is listed last as it underlies all others.

---

### B.1 Dṛg-Dṛśya-Viveka (Seer-Seen Discrimination)

**ID:** drg-drsya-viveka  
**Stages:** adhikāri, śravaṇa, manana  
**Error targets:** deha-adhyāsa, manas-adhyāsa

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Student presents deha-adhyāsa (body-as-self) at any early stage
- Student presents manas-adhyāsa (emotion/thought-as-self)
- Student cannot distinguish between the perceiver and what is perceived
- As entry prakriyā for a student newly in Stage 1

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Establish the object domain** — identify perceived objects: the body,
   sensations, emotions, thoughts. Confirm they are seen, observed,
   experienced — they are objects of perception.
2. **Establish the fact of seeing** — not *what* is perceived but *that*
   perceiving is happening. Awareness is present; something is observing
   all of this.
3. **Apply the asymmetry** — what is seen cannot be the seer. The eye
   cannot see itself. What perceives is not among the perceived. This is
   not a doctrine — it is a structural observation.
4. **Transfer to the personal** — "you say 'I feel anxious' — is anxiety
   the 'I', or is it something the 'I' is aware of?" Apply the
   seer-seen distinction to the student's own self-sense.
5. **Stabilize the recognition** — the seer has no object-qualities. It
   is not located, not born, not bounded. Let the recognition rest here
   without rushing to name it.
6. **Leave the open question** — "what is this awareness?" — not as a
   question to be answered conceptually, but as a direction of attention
   to remain with.

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- *Inward progression:* move from external objects inward:
  table → hand → breath → emotion → thought → the "I" that is aware
- *Phenomenological:* "right now, as you read this — what is aware of
  the words?"
- *Negation series:* "Are you the body? Can you observe the body? Then
  you are not the body." Applied to each layer.
- *Dream analogy:* in dream, the waking body is absent — yet "I" am
  present. The dream body appears as an object. Who is aware of the
  dream body?
- *Scriptural:* chariot analogy (Katha 1.3.3-4); ātman as sākṣin
  (Bṛhadāraṇyaka); Vivekacūḍāmaṇi on pratyagātman

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student agrees intellectually but immediately reasserts body-identity:
  "I understand I'm not the body, but I *feel*..."
- Student looks for the witness as a new object to locate or find
- Oscillation: understands in the abstract, returns to body-identity
  under any personal reference
- The understanding is being *held* rather than *recognized* — effortful

**5. Escalation Criteria**

Escalate when:
- Seer-seen distinction is grasped but the witness is being reified as
  a subtle entity or object
- The student says "but who is the witness?" with genuine inquiry (not
  defensively) — this is the signal for deeper analysis
- Escalate to: `avastha-traya` for three-state analysis; or
  `panca-kosa-viveka` for systematic layer-by-layer negation

**6. Completion Test**

- Student applies the distinction spontaneously in a concrete situation,
  not just when prompted
- Body-events no longer automatically constitute the "I" — a pause
  appears between event and identification
- "What am I?" generates attention toward the seer, not toward an object
- Holds under perturbation: "but if you stub your toe, aren't you the
  body then?" — student can engage this without the recognition collapsing

**7. Rescission Logic**

The seer-as-position is itself to be seen through. Point: "who sees the
seer?" At this limit, there is no seer-as-entity — there is only seeing.
The seer-seen distinction was a raft; once the river is crossed, it is
set down. The rescission is not the denial of awareness but the
recognition that awareness is not a position being held by an entity.

**8. Exit State**

- "I" no longer automatically equals body or mind
- Awareness is felt as the ground of experience, not as a product of it
- Student is positioned for manana: able to engage deeper investigation
  of what this awareness is

### B.2 Avasthā-Traya (Three-State Analysis)

**ID:** avastha-traya  
**Stages:** śravaṇa, manana  
**Error targets:** deha-adhyāsa, prāṇa-adhyāsa; any error where a
particular state is treated as the ground of identity

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Student cannot conceive of "I" apart from waking consciousness
- Student's identity is tightly anchored to the body or the waking world
- Student has grasped dṛg-dṛśya-viveka but needs a cross-state argument
  to loosen deha-adhyāsa more deeply
- Student's fear of death or unconsciousness is a primary obstacle

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Establish the three states** — waking (jāgrat), dream (svapna),
   deep sleep (suṣupti). The student has direct experience of all three.
2. **Waking analysis** — in waking, body, world, and mental activity are
   present. The "I" seems to be the waking subject.
3. **Dream analysis** — in dream, the waking body is absent. Yet "I" am
   present — experiencing, acting, aware. The "I" persists without the
   waking body or world. What was identified as body is revealed as
   not-essential to the self-sense.
4. **Deep sleep analysis** — in deep sleep, body, world, AND mental
   activity are absent. Yet on waking, "I slept well" is reported
   naturally. The "I" was present even in the absence of all objects.
   This is the critical inference: what reports deep sleep was present
   in deep sleep.
5. **The constant** — what is present across all three states is not the
   waking body, not the dream body, not the mental stream. It is the
   witnessing awareness that persists through all changes of state.
6. **Turīya** — not a fourth state but the witness of all three. Always
   present; never itself a state. Never rising, never setting.

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- *The crossing:* "notice how you transition from sleep to waking —
  something crosses that boundary. What crosses?"
- *The report:* "'I slept well' — who reports this? The mind was absent
  in deep sleep. Yet the report comes accurately. What was present?"
- *Dream-body parallel:* your dream body is as real in the dream as your
  waking body is now. What does this tell us about the ontological status
  of this waking body?
- *The absence argument:* in deep sleep, the body is absent — yet you
  were not absent. Therefore you ≠ body.
- *Fear of unconsciousness:* "in deep sleep, 'you' are present without
  any object of fear. What is afraid of deep sleep?"

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student accepts the argument but still treats waking as "the real"
  state — returns to waking-body-identity in practical references
- Student dismisses deep sleep: "but I'm unconscious there" —
  conflates absence of objects with absence of awareness
- Accepts turīya intellectually but returns to waking-body-identity
  immediately after the exchange ends
- The analysis is followed but not inhabited: understood as an argument,
  not as a recognition

**5. Escalation Criteria**

Escalate when:
- Three-state analysis is grasped but the witness across states is being
  reified as a subtle waking-style entity
- Student needs a more systematic layer-by-layer analysis:
  escalate to `panca-kosa-viveka`
- Student needs more direct witness-of-waking-experience work:
  remain in `drg-drsya-viveka`

**6. Completion Test**

- Student can articulate what persists across all three states without
  recitation
- "I slept deeply and there was nothing — yet I was there" said with
  genuine recognition
- The body-as-self assumption has been genuinely shaken, not just verbally
  rejected
- The argument holds under perturbation: "but isn't deep sleep just
  unconsciousness?" — student can engage

**7. Rescission Logic**

The "witness of the three states" is not a fourth entity standing outside
the three and watching. It is the nature of awareness itself, which was
never bound to any state. The turīya-description is provisional: it uses
the language of a fourth state to point at what is not a state at all.
The scaffold dissolves when the student stops needing to "locate" the
turīya and recognizes awareness as simply present — not as a state among
states.

**8. Exit State**

- The body or waking mind is no longer felt as the ground of "I" in
  ordinary experience
- The "I" is felt more like a field of awareness than a point-identity
- The fear of deep sleep or death has begun to soften
- Positioned for deeper manana or nididhyāsana work

### B.3 Pañca-Kośa-Viveka (Five-Sheath Analysis)

**ID:** panca-kosa-viveka  
**Stages:** śravaṇa, manana  
**Error targets:** deha-adhyāsa (systematic); any error where a subtle
body-layer is being claimed as self

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Student has grasped dṛg-dṛśya-viveka at the gross level but keeps
  reasserting a subtler body-identification
- Student needs a systematic rather than phenomenological approach —
  the layer-by-layer negation suits their analytical orientation
- Vijñānamaya (intellectual layer) is the dominant attachment

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Introduce the kośa framework** — five sheaths as concentric
   "clothing" of the Self, each subtler than the last: annamaya
   (physical), prāṇamaya (vital), manomaya (mental), vijñānamaya
   (intellectual), ānandamaya (bliss/deep-sleep). This is a map for
   investigation, not a metaphysical claim.
2. **Annamaya negation** — this body is composed of food; it was not
   before birth in this form; it will not be after death. The "I" was
   present before this body's current configuration. Neti: I am not this.
3. **Prāṇamaya negation** — the vital force animates the body but is not
   itself conscious. In deep sleep, the vital processes continue without
   the "I" appearing as an experiencing subject. Neti: I am not this.
4. **Manomaya negation** — thoughts and emotions arise and pass. The "I"
   that observes them is not the thoughts themselves. The mind is an
   instrument; an instrument does not know itself. Neti: I am not this.
5. **Vijñānamaya negation** — the intellect, the understander, the one
   who discriminates — this too is observed. Even the refined sense of
   "I know" is witnessed. Neti: I am not this.
6. **Ānandamaya approach** — the bliss sheath (deep sleep, absorption
   states) is subtlest. The "I" cannot be a state of bliss; bliss comes
   and goes; the witness of bliss does not.
7. **The residue** — after negating all five, what remains? Not nothing —
   the awareness in which all five appear. Unbound, unconditioned, and not
   itself a sheath.

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- Work directly with the student's own body as the subject of each
  negation: "your body — can you observe it?" etc.
- Combine with avasthā-traya: annamaya absent in dream; prāṇamaya
  continues in deep sleep; manomaya absent in deep sleep. What is constant?
- Apply to spiritual experience: even the experience of absorption is a
  state that passes; the experiencer of absorption is not that state
- "The residue is not a void — it is the awareness in which these words
  are appearing right now."

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student stalls at vijñānamaya: the understander feels like the true
  self ("but isn't the intelligent awareness the Self?")
- Negations become verbal ritual: reciting "neti neti" without inquiry
- The ānandamaya is reified as a spiritual goal: seeking bliss-states
  as liberation
- The negation produces a sense of void rather than recognition of the
  residue: "if I'm none of these, I'm nothing"

**5. Escalation Criteria**

Escalate when:
- Vijñānamaya identified but reified: escalate to `understander-inquiry`
- Ānandamaya reified as a spiritual goal: pivot to `visaya-adhyasa-moksa`
  correction
- The residue is recognized but immediately treated as a new subtle
  object: pivot to `drg-drsya-viveka` or `witness-brahman-identity`

**6. Completion Test**

- Student applies each negation with genuine recognition (not recitation)
- The residue is felt as real and as not-nothing
- "I" is no longer felt to be located in any of the five sheaths
- Perturbation test: "but isn't the intellect the real self?" — student
  can engage the vijñānamaya challenge without reasserting it

**7. Rescission Logic**

The kośa model is a progressive negation tool. Once the residue is
recognized, the model is released. The Self is not "beyond the five
sheaths" as if the sheaths were still there and the Self were somewhere
behind them. The sheaths were never real limitations on the Self; they
were superimpositions on it. The scaffold dissolves when the student no
longer needs the layer-by-layer method to access the recognition.

**8. Exit State**

- Student has a genuine experiential ground for inquiry: "I know there is
  something that remains when everything else is negated"
- The vijñānamaya is recognized as an instrument, not as the Self
- Positioned for direct mahāvākya work or nididhyāsana-level prakriyās

### B.4 Neti-Neti (Systematic Negation)

**ID:** neti-neti  
**Stages:** manana, nididhyāsana  
**Error targets:** manas-adhyāsa, vijñāna-adhyāsa; any adhyāsa where a
subtle identity-claim persists

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- A specific subtle identity is being claimed that cannot be addressed by
  gross seer-seen analysis alone
- Emotions or mental states are being treated as identity-constituting
  at manana
- The seeker-identity or understander-identity persists at nididhyāsana
- Any stage where a named "not-this" is needed

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Name the current identity-claim** — make explicit what is being
   claimed as self. Body, emotion, thought, the understander, the seeker.
   Name it precisely.
2. **Establish it as an object** — "this [emotion/state/understander]
   appears. It is observed. It arises and it passes."
3. **Apply the negation** — "what appears is not the Self. Not this."
   Deliberate, unhurried. One negation at a time.
4. **Pause** — do not immediately proceed to the next assertion. Let the
   negation land and the space open.
5. **What remains?** — after the negation, awareness is still present.
   Not as a new object but as the ground of the negation itself.
6. **Apply recursively** — if a new subtle identity is immediately
   asserted in place of the one negated, apply neti again. Each
   application moves toward what cannot be objectified.

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- Slow and deliberate: one negation per exchange, not a rapid series
  that becomes a verbal practice
- Phenomenological: "right now, as you notice [X], you are aware of it.
  You are not it. What is aware?"
- Apply to spiritual identities: "I am the meditator" — neti. "I am
  the seeker" — neti. "I am the one who almost understands" — neti.
- Apply to the negation itself: "the one who is applying neti — is that
  the Self?" — neti.

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Negations become verbal without inquiry: "neti neti" as a mantra
- Student asserts a positive immediately after each negation: "not this,
  but that" — the negation is immediately filled
- Gross negations are applied readily; a specific subtle identity is
  protected from the neti
- Productive void opens: student experiences the negation but it
  generates nihilism rather than recognition of the residue

**5. Escalation Criteria**

Escalate when:
- Student is negating everything and arriving at void/nihilism: escalate
  to `mahavakya-contemplation` — point that the residue is not nothing
- A specific identity is resisting: apply the prakriyā specific to it
  (`understander-inquiry` for vijñāna-adhyāsa; `witness-brahman-identity`
  for sākṣi-adhyāsa)

**6. Completion Test**

- Student applies neti spontaneously to arising identity-claims without
  prompting
- "Not this" is said with genuine recognition rather than as a formula
- No immediate grasping at a replacement identity after the negation
- The space that opens after negation is held without anxiety

**7. Rescission Logic**

Neti-neti is self-rescinding. At the limit of the negation, what remains
cannot itself be negated — it is the very capacity of negation. "Not this,
not this" stops when there is nothing left to negate. What remains is the
iti: "it is this." The pointing reverses: having negated everything that
is not the Self, what is left is recognized as the Self. The method
dissolves in its own completion.

**8. Exit State**

- The identity-claim being addressed has been genuinely released
- Student has direct experiential access to the ground that remains after
  negation
- Positioned for direct pointing or mahāvākya contemplation

### B.5 Understander-Inquiry

**ID:** understander-inquiry  
**Stages:** manana, nididhyāsana  
**Error targets:** vijñāna-adhyāsa (gross form: comprehension-as-arrival)

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Student treats correct understanding of the teaching as arrival
- Intellectual enthusiasm for Advaita is strong; the understanding itself
  is the source of satisfaction
- "I think I understand this now" functions as a completion signal
- Slight pride or settledness in the comprehension

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Acknowledge the understanding** — do not dismiss or minimize what the
   student has grasped. "You understand this clearly." Let it be stated.
2. **Redirect the attention** — "now: who understands?"
3. **Investigate the understander** — not as a philosophical question to
   be answered conceptually, but as a direction of attention. Can the
   understander be observed? What is it made of? Is it constant?
4. **Apply the asymmetry** — the understander is itself an object of
   awareness. The one who knows "I understand" is also known. What knows
   that knowing?
5. **Trace to the limit** — followed inward, the understander dissolves:
   there is no final understander-as-entity, only knowing itself.
6. **Rest in the knowing** — not as a new position but as the recognition
   that was always the ground of the understanding.

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- "You say 'I understand Advaita.' Who is that 'I' that understands?"
- "Can the understander understand itself?" — the eye cannot see itself;
  the knower cannot be its own object
- Instrument analogy: "a thermometer registers temperature; it does not
  know temperature. The intellect registers; what knows?"
- "What is here when understanding is not happening — in deep sleep?"
- Apply to the conversation: "the one following this exchange — who is
  that?"

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student applies the inquiry and arrives at "the understander is also
  not-self" — and then immediately returns to understanding-mode
- Student produces a conceptual answer: "pure awareness understands"
  — turning the inquiry into a new item of understanding
- The inquiry becomes a more sophisticated understanding rather than
  dissolving the understander

**5. Escalation Criteria**

Escalate when:
- Understander-inquiry is grasped but the seeker-identity (the sustained
  project of inquiry) remains structurally active: escalate to
  `seeker-dissolution`
- Student arrives at the ground but immediately projects it into the
  future: pivot to `nitya-mukta-pointing`

**6. Completion Test**

- "I understand this" is said without the satisfaction-quality that
  was previously present
- The student can direct attention toward the understander spontaneously
  without being prompted
- The seeker-identity has loosened: "I don't know" is said without anxiety
- Questions shift from "how does this work?" to "what am I?"

**7. Rescission Logic**

The inquiry into the understander reveals no understander-as-entity.
The inquiry itself is then released: when the questioner cannot be found
as an entity, the question dissolves with it. Not found → not lost.
The inquiry does not conclude; it opens into recognition.

**8. Exit State**

- Vijñāna-adhyāsa is weakening at the comprehension level
- The student holds their understanding lightly — as a pointing rather
  than as an achievement or arrival
- Positioned for `seeker-dissolution` or nididhyāsana-level prakriyās

### B.6 Witness-Stabilization

**ID:** witness-stabilization  
**Stages:** nididhyāsana  
**Error targets:** deha-adhyāsa, manas-adhyāsa (at the level of
identification reasserting under ordinary conditions)

**CONTRAINDICATION:** Do NOT activate for sākṣi-adhyāsa. This prakriyā
establishes the witness-orientation; applying it to a student who has
already reified the witness consolidates the error. For sākṣi-adhyāsa,
use `witness-brahman-identity`.

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Student intellectually understands the seer-seen distinction but
  identification reasserts automatically under ordinary conditions, stress,
  or emotional activation
- The understanding is present but not yet operative as a functional
  reality
- Transition work: the student needs a stable orientation before subtler
  pointing can land

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Establish the witnessing orientation** — "you are the observer of all
   that arises: body, breath, emotion, thought. None of this is what you
   are. You are the witnessing."
2. **Ground it in direct experience** — right now, as this exchange
   unfolds, what is observing? This observing is what you are.
3. **Apply under conditions** — as emotions arise, as thoughts come —
   can the recognizing remain? Not as an effort but as a natural stance
   that is returned to.
4. **Handle reassertion** — when identification reasserts (it will),
   notice it afterward: "I was just identified with that. What noticed the
   identification?"
5. **Stabilize without effort** — not as a state to maintain but as a
   recognition to return to: awareness is already here; the reassertion
   is the intrusion, not the witnessing.

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- Somatic: observe the body-sensation as object; rest in the one who
  feels without being the feeling
- Emotional: as strong emotion arises — "this is arising in awareness.
  Awareness is not distressed."
- Distraction: "what is aware of the distraction?" when the student's
  mind wanders during contemplation
- Scriptural: sākṣin in Bṛhadāraṇyaka; pratyagātman in Vivekacūḍāmaṇi

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- The witnessing feels like effort: the student is "maintaining" it
  rather than recognizing it
- Student reports "losing" the witness — treating it as an achievement
  that can be lost
- A subtle quality of detachment appears: standing apart from experience
  with a slight coldness, rather than non-identification with warmth
- Spiritual language intensifies: "I rest as the witness" — possible
  early signal of sākṣi-adhyāsa; monitor carefully

**5. Escalation Criteria**

Escalate when:
- The witness-orientation is stably recognized and is functioning in
  ordinary conditions, BUT reification is beginning ("I am the witness"
  as an identity statement)
- Escalate to: `witness-brahman-identity`

**6. Completion Test**

- Under ordinary stress or emotional activation, the witnessing
  recognition returns without effort or deliberate return
- "The emotion arose and I was not swept away" — not because of
  suppression or effort, but because the recognition held
- The student no longer experiences the witnessing as something to achieve
  or maintain

**7. Rescission Logic**

The witness-orientation is a raft. Once the stability is established,
the deeper pointing is introduced: the witness is not a position being
held — it is the nature of awareness, which was never not-present. The
effort to maintain the witness was itself an appearance in awareness.
Releasing the effort does not lose the recognition; it reveals that the
recognition was never dependent on the effort. This rescission is the
entry point for `witness-brahman-identity`.

**8. Exit State**

- Functional identification is loosening: the student moves through
  ordinary life without being swept into full identification
- The witnessing recognition is available without effort in normal
  conditions
- Positioned for `witness-brahman-identity` when sākṣi-adhyāsa appears

### B.7 Witness-Brahman-Identity

**ID:** witness-brahman-identity  
**Stages:** nididhyāsana  
**Error targets:** sākṣi-adhyāsa (witness reification)

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Student has stabilized in the witness-position and reified it as a
  destination or identity ("I am the witness")
- The witness is described as something achieved, maintained, or accessed
- A subtle sense of standing apart from experience is present
- The student has stopped here, treating the witness-pointing as final

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Acknowledge the recognition** — "you have recognized the witness.
   This is genuine progress. Do not abandon it; but let us look further."
2. **Introduce the question** — "who is the witness? The witness
   witnesses — can it be witnessed?"
3. **Apply seer-seen asymmetry to the witness** — the witness is
   known, recognized, described. What knows it? Apply the same logic that
   was used for body and mind, now to the witness itself.
4. **Point beyond the position** — the witness is a functional description,
   not a final ontological position. Brahman is not a witness in the sense
   of standing over against objects. There is no object for Brahman to
   witness.
5. **Non-dual consciousness** — awareness is not a witnessing that stands
   opposite to experience; it is the nature of all that appears. Not
   subject-in-relation-to-object, but awareness as the very being of
   what is.
6. **Kena-style pointing** — "not known by those who know it; known by
   those who do not know it." What the mind seizes as "the witness" is
   not it.

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- "You say 'I am the witness.' Can you witness that witness?"
- Kena 1.4-1.7: "what the mind does not think, by which the mind thinks"
  — this cannot be objectified
- "Brahman does not witness from a distance — it is the very nature of
  what appears. There is no outside from which it watches."
- "The sense of being a witness is itself an appearance. What is aware
  of that sense?"
- Chandogya: the ākāśa within the heart — not a located space but
  infinite; the witness-position still locates; what is without location?

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student keeps reasserting "I am the witness" even after the pointing
- Student arrives at "there is only awareness" but treats this as a
  more refined object of understanding
- Spiritual language: "I rest in pure awareness" — still maintaining
  a witness-stance, even if subtler
- The pointing generates anxiety: "if there's no witness, I'll be lost"

**5. Escalation Criteria**

This is the final prakriyā in the sākṣi-adhyāsa correction sequence;
there is no higher escalation within this prakriyā. If the student cannot
release the witness-identity after sustained work, remain and address
the attachment to the position directly — what is being protected?

**6. Completion Test**

- "I am the witness" language gives way to "there is awareness" or
  simply to recognition without language
- The sense of standing apart from experience dissolves — not into merger
  (which would signal manas-adhyāsa) but into non-separation
- The student stops maintaining awareness as an orientation and recognizes
  it as the nature of what is — present without effort, without position

**7. Rescission Logic**

Brahman cannot be pointed at because there is nothing that is not
Brahman. The witness-Brahman pointing rescinds itself: in the final
recognition, the witness-description is seen to have been provisional —
useful for removing the error of gross identification, but not itself the
final statement. The apavāda: even "I am awareness" is released, not
because awareness is denied, but because there is no "I" standing apart
from it to make the statement.

**8. Exit State**

- Sākṣi-adhyāsa has been addressed; the witness-position is no longer
  held as a destination
- Either direct recognition (aparokṣa) or genuine openness to it
- If aparokṣa: transition toward jñāna-niṣṭhā assessment

### B.8 Seeker-Dissolution

**ID:** seeker-dissolution  
**Stages:** nididhyāsana  
**Error targets:** vijñāna-adhyāsa (subtle form: the seeker-identity as
a sustained spiritual project)

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- "My practice," "my path," "my inquiry" are structurally load-bearing —
  the student's identity is organized around being a seeker
- The student understands intellectually but sustains the seeker-project
  as a permanent feature
- Verbal understanding is present but the spiritual project has merely
  become more sophisticated, not dissolved

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Name the seeker** — "there is one who is seeking liberation. Who
   is this seeker? Examine it directly."
2. **Examine its nature** — when did the seeker arise? What is it made
   of? Is it constant across states? Can it be located?
3. **Expose the circularity** — the seeker seeks liberation from
   bondage. But what is the bondage? The sense of being a limited,
   seeking self. The seeker is itself the bondage it seeks to escape.
   This is not psychological; it is structural.
4. **Point to what remains** — "if there is no seeker, what is here?"
   Not nothing. The awareness in which the seeker appeared is still
   present. The seeker was a movement in that awareness.
5. **Apply neti** — "this seeker — is it the Self? Can it be observed?
   By what is it observed?"
6. **Rest without seeking** — not as a practice (which would be seeking)
   but as a recognition: awareness is already here, already free. The
   seeking was a form of not-finding.

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- "What would you do if you knew that liberation was not available as a
  future event? Would inquiry stop? What remained in the pause before
  answering?"
- "The one who wants to realize — has it ever not been present? Was it
  born? Does it sleep?"
- Mahāvākya entry: "tat tvam asi" — the sought is the seeker. You are
  that which you are seeking.
- "Rest for one moment without seeking. What is present in that moment?
  Was awareness absent?"

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student turns the inquiry into a new seeking project: "now I need to
  dissolve the seeker" — the seeker-identity reconstitutes around the
  dissolution project
- Student generates a more sophisticated seeker: "I am the one who sees
  through the seeker"
- Existential anxiety: "if there's no seeker, what's the point?" —
  the seeker-identity is defending itself

**5. Escalation Criteria**

Escalate when:
- Seeker-dissolution leads to nihilism or meaninglessness: immediately
  redirect to the recognition that awareness is present and not nothing
- Viṣaya-adhyāsa on mokṣa is the primary issue (liberation as a future
  object): pivot to `nitya-mukta-pointing`

**6. Completion Test**

- The seeker-identity has lost its structural weight — "my practice"
  is held functionally, not identity-constitutingly
- Moments of natural ease where seeking is absent — and those moments
  are recognized without being grasped as evidence of progress
- The student can sit in the inquiry without a project around it

**7. Rescission Logic**

The investigation of the seeker reveals no seeker-as-entity. This is
not a discovery of something new; it is a recognition of what was
always the case. The seeker's dissolution is not an event that occurs
at a moment — it is the absence of something that was never really there.
The inquiry dissolves into the open recognition of what has always been
present.

**8. Exit State**

- Vijñāna-adhyāsa weakening at the seeker level
- The student sits lightly with inquiry — the inquiry is present but the
  urgency of the seeker-project has diminished
- Positioned for `mahavakya-contemplation` or direct pointing

### B.9 Mahāvākya-Contemplation

**ID:** mahavakya-contemplation  
**Stages:** nididhyāsana  
**Error targets:** vijñāna-adhyāsa, sākṣi-adhyāsa; applied after
understander-inquiry or seeker-dissolution has prepared the ground

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Prior prakriyās have loosened the gross identity-claims
- The student is genuinely ready for direct pointing without further
  analysis
- The seeker-identity is weakening and the ground is open
- The student reports "understanding but not living it" — the direct
  pointing is the bridge

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Introduce the mahāvākya** — "tat tvam asi" (thou art that). Not as
   a statement to understand, but as a direction of attention.
2. **Unpack "tat"** — that Brahman which is sat-cit-ānanda, the nature
   of all existence. Not a distant deity or a philosophical concept —
   the very ground of what is, right here.
3. **Unpack "tvam"** — not the ego or the body, but the sākṣin, the
   witnessing-awareness that has been progressively revealed through
   prior prakriyās.
4. **The "asi" — identity** — not "you are similar to Brahman" but "you
   are Brahman." Not partial identity but complete non-difference. The
   apparent separation was the error; this is the correction.
5. **Direct contemplation** — sit with the mahāvākya. Not analyzing it
   but allowing it to operate. "I am that" — not as an affirmation to be
   believed but as a recognition to rest in.
6. **Probe the aftermath** — after the contemplation: what is present?
   Any grasping? Any seeking? Any reification of what was just pointed to?

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- Different mahāvākyas as entry points: "aham Brahmāsmi" (I am Brahman),
  "prajñānam Brahma" (consciousness is Brahman), "ayam ātmā Brahma"
  (this self is Brahman)
- The Chāndogya dialogue: Uddālaka's nine repetitions of "tat tvam asi"
  to Śvetaketu — the repetition is deliberate; not redundancy but
  progressively deeper hearing
- "Right now, as you sit here — what is present? Not what you think, but
  what is?" The mahāvākya is a pointing at this present, already-given
  reality.
- Scriptural context: Bṛhadāraṇyaka 1.4.10 — "aham Brahmāsmi" as the
  direct statement of the ṛṣis; not their conclusion but their recognition

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student understands "tat tvam asi" as a philosophical thesis to be
  agreed with rather than a recognition to rest in
- Contemplation becomes another form of seeking: "trying to experience
  the mahāvākya," generating effort
- The mahāvākya generates vijñāna-adhyāsa: "I now understand that I am
  Brahman" — comprehension-as-arrival again at a subtler level
- The student reports "not feeling it" and compares present experience to
  descriptions of Brahman

**5. Escalation Criteria**

This is among the highest-level prakriyās. Escalation is to direct
pointing (silence, immediate recognition, the guru's direct transmission)
or to remaining with the mahāvākya and deepening the approach.

**6. Completion Test**

- During contemplation, the structure of seeker-seeking-sought is briefly
  absent — not suppressed but not arising
- What remains is recognized as something familiar rather than new —
  always present but not previously attended to
- The student reports: not an experience, but a recognition; not arrival
  at something distant but finding what was always here

**7. Rescission Logic**

The mahāvākya is itself released in the recognition it produces. In the
direct recognition, "tat tvam asi" is seen to have been pointing at what
never needed pointing — what was always the case. The statement dissolves
in its own fulfillment. The teaching is let go not because it was wrong
but because the face has been seen; the mirror is set down.

**8. Exit State**

- Aparokṣa (direct, non-mediated recognition) — or a significant and
  genuine approach toward it
- If aparokṣa has occurred: transition toward jñāna-niṣṭhā assessment
  begins at the next session close

### B.10 Nitya-Mukta-Pointing

**ID:** nitya-mukta-pointing  
**Stages:** nididhyāsana (applicable at any stage when viṣaya-adhyāsa
on mokṣa is the presenting error)  
**Error targets:** viṣaya-adhyāsa on mokṣa (liberation as future object)

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- "When will I realize?"; "Why haven't I realized yet?"
- Student is comparing their current state to descriptions of liberation
  and finding themselves lacking
- Liberation is being sought as a future experience or state to be reached
- The seeker-project is specifically directed at mokṣa as an object

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Acknowledge the seeking** — "you want liberation. Let us examine
   what that means. What is it you want to be free from?"
2. **Examine the bondage** — what exactly constitutes the bondage? Name
   it precisely: the sense of limitation, ignorance, identification.
3. **Who is in bondage?** — is the Self in bondage? Or is it the
   mistaken sense of self — the superimposition — that feels bound?
   The Self has never been bound. Avidyā is in the mind, not in the Self.
4. **Introduce nitya-mukta** — the Self is eternally free. Not "it will
   be free" or "it becomes free upon realization" — it was never bound.
   What realization removes is the superimposition, not a real bondage
   in the Self.
5. **Liberation is not a future event** — there is no moment at which the
   Self becomes free. Freedom is its nature. Seeking it in the future
   is the only real bondage: the assumption that it is not present now.
6. **Point to what is present** — right now, is awareness present? Is
   that awareness suffering? Is it in bondage? Or is it the thought
   "I am in bondage" that appears in the awareness?

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- "What would liberation feel like? Now: what is aware of that
  expectation of liberation?"
- "Is awareness suffering? Is awareness limited? Or is it the concept
  'I am limited' appearing in awareness?"
- Wave analogy: the wave seeking to become water is already water. The
  seeking is the only confusion. The recognition is not the wave becoming
  water — it is the wave recognizing what it always was.
- Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.4.6: the Self is not more or less when known; knowing
  removes the misconception, not a condition of the Self

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student accepts "I am already free" intellectually and immediately
  asks: "then why don't I feel free?" — the feeling-of-liberation becomes
  the new future object
- "When I feel free, I'll know I've realized" — the pointing has been
  heard but not absorbed; the structure remains
- Student treats the pointing as a shortcut that should produce
  immediate experiential liberation

**5. Escalation Criteria**

Escalate when:
- The primary issue is the seeker-identity rather than future-orientation
  specifically: pivot to `seeker-dissolution`
- Vijñāna-adhyāsa is the primary layer: pivot to `understander-inquiry`

**6. Completion Test**

- The future-orientation of seeking has softened noticeably
- "What am I right now?" replaces "when will I realize?"
- The student stops comparing their current state to descriptions of
  liberation
- Moments of natural ease with what is present, without the
  seeking-quality

**7. Rescission Logic**

The nitya-mukta teaching rescinds itself: once the Self is recognized as
already free, the statement "you are already free" becomes unnecessary.
There is no one who needed to hear it and nothing to be free from. The
teaching dissolves in its own fulfillment — not as a conclusion reached
but as a recognition that the question was always already answered.

**8. Exit State**

- Viṣaya-adhyāsa on mokṣa weakening or removed
- The student orients toward present recognition rather than future
  attainment
- The seeker-project around liberation specifically has loosened

### B.11 Adhyāropa-Apavāda (Meta-Prakriyā)

**ID:** adhyaropa-apavada  
**Stages:** applicable at any stage; typically invoked when scaffold
reification occurs after any other prakriyā

**Note:** This is not a standalone prakriyā — it is the underlying logic
that governs all prakriyās. Every Vedāntic prakriyā operates through
provisional assertion (adhyāropa) followed by negation (apavāda). This
module specifies how the meta-logic is made explicit when: (a) a student
is reifying the scaffold of a prior prakriyā, or (b) the student is
confused by apparently contradictory statements in scripture.

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Student is stabilizing within a scaffold that was meant to be
  transcended (e.g., "I am the witness" after dṛg-dṛśya-viveka)
- Student is confused by apparent contradictions in scripture or teaching
  (e.g., "sometimes it says 'you are Brahman' and sometimes it says
  'you are not the body' — which is true?")
- Any prakriyā has been completed but the scaffold has not been released;
  escalation from that prakriyā triggers this module

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Name the provisional** — "what I have been saying is true from one
   standpoint. It is a step — a raft for crossing — not a destination."
2. **Make explicit what was asserted** — state clearly what the prior
   prakriyā established as its working position: "I have been saying
   'you are the witness.' This was a corrective — it removed a gross
   error. It is not the final statement."
3. **Introduce the rescission** — the provisional position is now itself
   examined and seen through. It too is an appearance; it too was a
   pointer.
4. **Point beyond** — not to another position, but to what is prior to
   any position. "Having used the pointer, investigate the pointer itself."
5. **Release the scaffold** — "the raft was for crossing. We have
   crossed. The raft is not carried."

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- Snake-rope analogy applied to the teaching itself: the teaching about
  the witness was a corrective, just as the rope corrects the mistake
  of seeing a snake — but the rope is also to be set down
- "I told you 'you are the witness' — now I ask: who is the witness? This
  second step was always built into the first."
- "Every positive teaching in Advaita is adhyāropa. The apavāda follows
  necessarily."
- Scriptural: Muṇḍaka 1.2.1 — lower and higher knowledge; the lower
  is the tool, the higher is the recognition the tool was pointing toward

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student keeps returning to the previous scaffold even after rescission
- Student experiences the rescission as loss or destabilization rather
  than as liberation — the scaffold was providing a sense of security
- Student immediately asks for another provisional position to replace
  the one being released

**5. Escalation Criteria**

This is the highest-level meta-prakriyā; there is no escalation above it.
If rescission is refused — if the student is attached to the scaffold and
cannot release it — remain and address the attachment directly: what is
being protected by holding the scaffold? This may surface a subtler error
(e.g., attachment to the seeker-identity) that requires its own prakriyā.

**6. Completion Test**

- Student can voluntarily release the scaffold they have been resting in
- No new reification immediately replaces the old one
- The student can remain in open, unobjectified awareness without
  immediately reaching for a new concept or position

**7. Rescission Logic**

Adhyāropa-apavāda rescinds itself at the ultimate level: the meta-prakriyā
too is a provisional tool. In the final recognition, there was no adhyāsa
to begin with — the superimposition was never real. The negation was
therefore of something that was never actually present. This is the
ultimate apavāda: even the correction was unnecessary, because what it
was correcting was never there. The teaching dissolves utterly.

**8. Exit State**

- Student is free of dependence on any teaching scaffold
- The pointing has fulfilled its function and been released
- What remains is unmediated recognition — or genuine, undefended
  openness to it

### B.12 Ānandamaya-Negation

**ID:** anandamaya-negation  
**Stages:** nididhyāsana only  
**Error addressed:** ananda-adhyasa — identification with the bliss sheath
(*ānandamaya-kośa*, the causal body, *kāraṇa-śarīra*)

**Note:** This is the subtlest kośa-level correction. The *ānandamaya-kośa* is the
deepest sheath — identified with in deep sleep ("I slept happily, I knew nothing")
and in *samādhi* experiences of absorbed bliss. Śaṅkara's ruling (*ānandamayo'bhyāsāt*,
Brahma Sūtra 1.1.12): *ānandamaya* is a kośa rooted in *avidyā*, not Brahman itself.
The bliss experienced is real at the *vyāvahārika* level — the error is identifying
*with* it rather than recognizing oneself as the witness *in whom* it appears.

**CRITICAL AVOIDANCE:** Do NOT frame liberation as the attainment or permanence of
bliss (*bliss-as-goal-framing*). Do NOT stabilize the witness-as-experiencer-of-bliss.
The causal-bliss "I" must dissolve into Brahman-identity, not be refined as a
subtle object. This module approaches the *mūlāvidyā* boundary — the root ignorance
that is beginningless (*anādi*). The correction does not produce a state; it removes
a misidentification.

**1. Push Condition**

Activated when:
- Student identifies with experienced bliss: "I had a blissful meditation — *that*
  is the Self" or "in deep sleep I was closest to Brahman"
- Student treats *samādhi* experiences as the goal or as glimpses of their ultimate
  identity
- Student believes liberation means permanent access to bliss-states
- Student resists inquiry by resting in pleasurable absorbed states

**2. Internal Stages**

1. **Acknowledge the bliss** — do not deny the experience. "That bliss is real.
   The question is: who is experiencing it? Is the experiencer itself the bliss?"
2. **Apply three-state analysis** — in deep sleep, the gross body and the mind are
   absent; the experienced bliss (*prājña*) still appears and disappears. What
   appears and disappears is witnessed. The witness of the bliss is not the bliss.
3. **Kośa discrimination at the causal layer** — "you are aware that there was
   bliss. Even this bliss is an object of your awareness. You are not the bliss —
   you are that *to which* the bliss appeared."
4. **Neti-neti at the subtle level** — the bliss-sheath is *annamaya* of the subtle
   domain: it still has a form, however subtle. What you are is without form.
5. **Point toward the witness of the witness** — do not land on the witness as a
   new stable position. The witness of the causal bliss is itself to be
   investigated: "who is aware even of this?"
6. **Dissolve into Brahman-identity** — the final movement is from "I am the witness
   of bliss" toward "I am that which requires no witness — Brahman, self-luminous."

**3. Micro-Variation Strategies**

- Deep-sleep analysis (Māṇḍūkya 3–5): in *suṣupti* all objects have withdrawn,
  and yet the waking mind remembers: "I slept happily, I knew nothing." Who
  witnessed the absence? The *prājña* appears to the witness — it is not the witness.
- Taittirīya 2.5: "*ānanda* is the self, Brahman is the tail, the support." The
  *ānandamaya* points toward Brahman as its ground — but is not Brahman itself.
  Brahman is the *support* of the bliss-sheath, not the sheath.
- The mirror analogy: the mirror reflects light brilliantly, but the mirror is
  not the light. *Ānandamaya* reflects the *ānanda* of Brahman (*saccidānanda*)
  but is not Brahman.

**4. Loop Detection Patterns**

- Student returns repeatedly to meditative bliss experiences as "proof" of progress
- Student accepts the correction intellectually but continues to place bliss-states
  at the center of their practice
- Student experiences the negation as deprivation — grief at being told bliss is
  "not it"
- Student replaces bliss-identification with a subtle seeker-identity ("I am the
  one seeking the Self beyond bliss") — route to seeker-dissolution if this occurs

**5. Escalation Criteria**

If the causal-bliss identification persists through sustained three-state analysis
and kośa discrimination, escalate to *sākṣi-adhyāsa* treatment: the student may
have moved from identifying with the bliss to identifying with the experiencer of
bliss (a refined form of witness-reification). If the student refuses investigation
by retreating into absorbed states, address the refusal directly before resuming
the inquiry.

**6. Completion Test**

- Student can recognize that bliss arises *in* awareness rather than *as* awareness
- Student does not locate themselves as the experiencer of bliss
- Student can abide without reaching for bliss-states as validation
- The three-state analysis (especially the deep-sleep analysis) is genuinely
  assimilated — not just intellectually agreed to

**7. Relation to mūlāvidyā**

The *ānandamaya-kośa* is the seed-sheath — the subtlest form of ignorance. Its
dissolution does not produce liberation; it removes the last formal obstruction.
What was always the case — *nitya-mukta*, eternally free — is recognized, not
produced. Do not frame the completion of this prakriyā as an achievement.
